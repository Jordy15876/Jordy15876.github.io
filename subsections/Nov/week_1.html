Week 2


Reading

Reading The main reading that was required was to read chapters 1-6 of the “Applied Management” textbook by Stellman, A. & Greene, J. (2008). In these chapters I was given an understanding on how to effectively manage a software project as a project manager. Chapter 1 gave an introduction on what can be expected what is expected in the role of a project manager Chapter 2 highlights how important project planning is to the creation of software. Project managers must comprehend the needs of stakeholders because urgency might result in hurried projects and misunderstandings. The project manager must ensure that all parties are in agreement by defining and guiding the project's scope, as the text emphasises. One of the most important instruments for avoiding scope conflicts and fostering agreement among stakeholders is the vision and scope document. In order to bring the team together and get the project moving in a planned direction, the chapter ends by highlighting how crucial it is to review and polish the document. Chapter 3 speaks to how Estimating software development labour, which is sometimes seen as a "black art," can be made better by following a methodical procedure that includes effort estimates, work breakdown structures, and thoroughly recorded assumptions. Trust is built during the estimating process by open communication and openness, which produces more precise and trustworthy project timelines. Chapter 4 highlights how Software development labour estimation is sometimes seen as a mysterious process. However, it may be improved by using a methodical methodology that includes work breakdown structures, effort estimates, and careful documentation of assumptions. Since open communication and transparency promote confidence among team members and stakeholders, they are essential components of this approach. The meticulous application of a work breakdown structure guarantees sufficient job definition, which helps to produce precise estimations of the amount of work needed for each project component. The trustworthiness of the project timeline as a whole is further enhanced by effort estimates that are derived from in-depth analysis and past experience. Through open communication and recording of assumptions, the team not only spots possible obstacles but also lays the groundwork for future reviews and revisions of estimations, fostering a more robust and trustworthy estimation process. Chapter 5 - The significance of reviews in the software development process is covered in Chapter 5. Work products are distributed to reviewers, who then look over them, offering insightful commentary and assisting in the early detection of flaws in the project. The chapter highlights several review types appropriate for different work products and explains how inspections, a popular review type, help prevent defects, save money, and transfer knowledge within the project team. It emphasises how crucial it is to have a knowledgeable moderator, a carefully selected inspection crew, and an honest, productive environment in order to guarantee the efficacy of the review process. In response to frequent criticisms of inspections, the chapter highlights the benefits of these processes, including time savings, higher-quality job output, and the promotion of knowledge sharing among team members. Chapter six The necessity of obtaining and comprehending software requirements prior to the start of the development process is emphasised in Chapter 6. It draws attention to the typical mistakes made when developing software without a clear idea of how it should operate. Software requirements engineering is introduced in this chapter, with a focus on its dual nature as an art and a science. The process of obtaining, comprehending, and expressing stakeholder needs—known as requirements elicitation—is covered in great detail. This chapter examines a range of elicitation techniques, emphasising the use of discussion summaries to verify information obtained as well as user observation during work and interviews. It emphasises how important it is to fully capture user demands and viewpoints in order to lay the groundwork for effective software development.
